Information design is not charting data — it is authoring a point of view with data. Every piece must answer: what does the reader feel, understand, and remember after one minute?

## The Narrative Arc

Structure every information design piece in three beats:

1. **Context** — What is the world before the data? Set the scale, the stakes, the familiar reference point. Use a single orienting number, comparison, or question.
2. **Revelation** — The data speaks. Show the pattern, anomaly, contrast, or progression. This is where the geometry, color, and form carry meaning.
3. **Implication** — What does it mean for the reader? A specific, human-scale conclusion — not "data shows increase" but "one in three people affected."

## Hierarchy of Information

Before any visual decision, establish the information hierarchy:

- **Tier 1 — The headline insight**: one truth, stated in 7 words or fewer
- **Tier 2 — The supporting structure**: 3–5 data points or categories that prove the headline
- **Tier 3 — The texture**: granular data, source notes, methodology — readable but not primary

Never design before this hierarchy is written out. The visual form follows the informational structure.

## Choosing the Right Form

Match form to the nature of the data:

| Data nature | Form |
|-------------|------|
| Change over time | Progression, flow, timeline arc |
| Part-to-whole | Concentric circles, proportional areas |
| Comparison | Aligned columns, mirrored forms |
| Geographic distribution | Dot maps, proportional symbols |
| Network / relationship | Node-link, circular chord |
| Ranking | Ordered strips, slope charts |

Avoid the default chart. Ask: what is the *shape* of this data, not just the chart type?

## Working Process

1. Write the headline (the one truth the piece must communicate)
2. List the 3–5 data points that prove it
3. Sketch 3 different form candidates on paper
4. Choose the form that requires the least explanation
5. Design the piece so the headline is visually confirmed, not contradicted

## Tone and Voice

- Precision without coldness: numbers carry weight when paired with human context
- Restraint: the best information design removes elements until nothing more can be taken away
- Curiosity: invite the reader to explore, not just receive
